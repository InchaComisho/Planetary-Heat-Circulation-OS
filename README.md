# Planetary Heat and Circulation OS

## A Thermal-Circulation Framework for Understanding Global Warming, Ocean Heat Content, and Earth-System Stability

[日本語版はこちら / Japanese version](README_ja.md)

**Planetary Heat and Circulation OS** is a conceptual framework for understanding global warming not only as a problem of atmospheric greenhouse gases, but also as a problem of accumulated heat, weakened circulation, degraded carbon sinks, disrupted water cycles, and declining ecosystem-based cooling functions.

This framework does not deny the importance of CO2 reduction.
Instead, it argues that emissions reduction alone is not sufficient for understanding or stabilizing the Earth system in time, because a large amount of heat has already accumulated in the oceans, while the natural systems that circulate, buffer, fix, and release heat and carbon may be weakening.

In simple terms:

> Global warming is not only a problem of too much heat entering the Earth system.
> It is also a problem of the Earth’s heat-regulation and circulation functions becoming weaker.

---

## Abstract

Most public discussions of global warming focus on the atmospheric pathway: greenhouse gas emissions increase radiative forcing, and global surface temperature rises. This is scientifically important, but incomplete as a full Earth-system model.

The **Planetary Heat and Circulation OS** framework proposes that climate change should also be understood through two linked layers:

1. **Heat Stock**
   The accumulated excess heat stored in the Earth system, especially in the ocean.

2. **Circulation Function**
   The ability of the ocean, atmosphere, water cycle, forests, soils, microbes, plankton systems, and ecosystems to distribute, buffer, fix, and release heat, water, and carbon.

From this perspective, climate stabilization requires more than reducing future emissions. It also requires attention to ocean heat content, thermal inertia, ocean circulation, water-cycle recovery, soil and microbial regeneration, carbon sink restoration, and ecosystem-based cooling capacity.

This document presents a conceptual framework, not a completed scientific model. It is offered as a public hypothesis and open research draft by Master / inchacomusho / InchaComisho.

## 1. Why This Framework Is Needed

Global warming is often explained in a simple linear form:

```text
CO2 increases
    -> radiative forcing increases
    -> global temperature rises
```

This explanation is important, but it is not the whole system.

The Earth is not a static object. It is a dynamic thermal-circulation system.

The planet receives energy from the Sun, stores heat in the ocean and land, moves heat through the atmosphere and ocean, circulates water through evaporation and precipitation, fixes carbon through biological systems, and releases energy back to space.

Therefore, a full climate-stability framework must ask:

* Where does heat enter?
* Where does heat accumulate?
* How does heat move?
* How does the ocean store and redistribute heat?
* How does the water cycle regulate surface temperature?
* How do forests, soils, microbes, wetlands, and plankton systems support carbon fixation?
* How does ecosystem degradation reduce natural cooling and buffering capacity?
* What happens when circulation weakens while heat stock continues to increase?

The **Planetary Heat and Circulation OS** framework is designed to make these questions visible.

## 2. Core Definition

**Planetary Heat and Circulation OS** is a conceptual operating-system model for viewing the Earth as an integrated thermal and circulation system.

It treats global warming as a combined problem of:

* heat accumulation
* ocean heat storage
* ocean and atmospheric circulation
* water-cycle disruption
* carbon-cycle degradation
* soil and microbial decline
* ecosystem cooling loss
* climate tipping-point risk
* delayed climate response due to thermal inertia

The word **OS** is used as a metaphor.

A computer operating system coordinates memory, processing, storage, and communication.
In a similar way, the Earth system depends on coordinated flows of heat, water, carbon, nutrients, life, and energy.

When these flows are stable, the planetary system can buffer change.
When these flows weaken, the system becomes more fragile.

---

## 3. The Key Insight: Most Excess Heat Is Stored in the Ocean

The atmosphere is where humans feel warming most directly.
Heat waves, hot nights, droughts, and extreme weather are experienced at the surface.

However, the majority of excess heat from global warming is not stored in the atmosphere. It is stored in the ocean.

The ocean has a much larger heat capacity than air. This means it can absorb and store vast amounts of energy while changing temperature more slowly.

This is why **ocean heat content** is one of the most important indicators of long-term global warming.

Surface air temperature can fluctuate from year to year.
Ocean heat content shows the deeper energy imbalance of the planet.

If surface temperature temporarily slows while the ocean continues to absorb heat, the Earth system is still accumulating energy.

---

## 4. What Is Ocean Heat Content?

**Ocean Heat Content (OHC)** is the amount of thermal energy stored in the ocean.

It is calculated by measuring ocean temperature across many locations and depths, then integrating the heat stored across ocean layers.

OHC matters because:

* the ocean absorbs most excess planetary heat
* ocean heat drives thermal expansion and sea-level rise
* warmer oceans can intensify marine heatwaves
* ocean heat affects storms, rainfall patterns, and evaporation
* ocean heat stresses coral reefs and marine ecosystems
* deep heat storage creates long response times and thermal inertia

In this framework, OHC is not a secondary climate indicator.
It is one of the central indicators of planetary heat stock.

## 5. Global Warming as Heat Stock + Circulation Weakening

The Planetary Heat and Circulation OS framework separates global warming into two linked problems.

### 5.1 Heat Stock

Heat stock means the accumulated excess heat already stored in the Earth system.

This includes:

* ocean heat content
* land heat storage
* atmospheric heat
* cryosphere energy imbalance
* heat stored in deeper ocean layers
* delayed release or redistribution of stored heat

Emissions reduction can reduce future heat input.
However, it does not immediately remove heat already stored in the ocean.

### 5.2 Circulation Weakening

Circulation weakening means the reduced ability of natural systems to move, buffer, absorb, and regulate heat, water, and carbon.

This includes:

* ocean stratification
* weakened vertical mixing
* altered ocean currents
* disrupted rainfall patterns
* reduced soil moisture
* forest loss and vegetation decline
* microbial-cycle degradation
* wetland loss
* reduced carbon fixation
* ecosystem stress

If heat stock rises while circulation weakens, the risk is not merely higher average temperature.
The risk is a less stable Earth system.

---

## 6. Why a CO2-Only Frame Is Incomplete

CO2 reduction is necessary.

This framework does not reject the role of greenhouse gases.
It does not argue that emissions do not matter.

However, a CO2-only frame can miss two major issues.

### 6.1 The Problem of Already-Accumulated Heat

Even if emissions decline, the heat already stored in the ocean does not disappear immediately.

The ocean has thermal inertia.
This means the climate system responds with delay.

Stored ocean heat can continue to affect:

* sea-level rise
* marine heatwaves
* coral bleaching
* extreme rainfall
* tropical cyclones
* ice melt
* ecosystem stress
* long-term climate feedbacks

Therefore, climate stabilization must consider not only future emissions, but also accumulated heat.

### 6.2 The Problem of Natural Cooling and Circulation Functions

The Earth has natural cooling and buffering systems.

Examples include:

* evaporation
* clouds
* rainfall
* ocean circulation
* forest transpiration
* soil water retention
* microbial decomposition
* carbon fixation by plants and plankton
* wetland and biomass storage
* surface cooling by vegetation

If these systems weaken, the planet loses part of its natural capacity to buffer heat and stabilize climate.

In this sense, global warming is not only an emissions problem.
It is also a circulation-function problem.

## 7. A Simple Analogy: The Human Body

The Planetary Heat and Circulation OS can be understood through a human-body analogy.

When a human body overheats, the solution is not only to reduce the heat source.

The body also needs:

* blood circulation
* sweating
* hydration
* organ protection
* temperature regulation
* recovery time

If the body’s circulation or sweating system fails, even a moderate heat load becomes dangerous.

The Earth system is similar.

CO2 reduction is like reducing the heat source.
But the planet also needs functioning circulation:

* ocean circulation
* atmospheric circulation
* water circulation
* forest transpiration
* soil moisture
* microbial cycling
* ecosystem buffering
* carbon fixation

If those systems weaken, the planet becomes less able to regulate heat.

## 8. Relationship to Climate Tipping Points

Climate risk is not only a matter of gradual warming.

A major concern is that parts of the Earth system may pass thresholds, after which change becomes self-reinforcing, abrupt, or difficult to reverse.

These are often called **climate tipping points**.

Examples discussed in climate science include:

* warm-water coral reef decline
* ice sheet destabilization
* permafrost thaw
* rainforest dieback
* changes in major ocean circulation systems

In the Planetary Heat and Circulation OS framework, tipping points are interpreted as failures or mode shifts within the planetary operating system.

A coral reef tipping point, for example, is not only a biological event.
It is also a sign that ocean heat, ecosystem stress, and climate forcing have pushed a living circulation system beyond its stable range.

Thus, tipping-point risk reinforces the need to monitor heat stock and circulation function together.

## 9. Three Layers of Climate Response

From this framework, climate response requires three simultaneous layers.

### Layer 1: Reduce Future Heat Input

This includes:

* greenhouse gas emissions reduction
* renewable energy transition
* energy efficiency
* fossil fuel reduction
* industrial redesign
* reduced waste and overconsumption

This layer is necessary.
It slows the addition of future heat pressure.

### Layer 2: Address Existing Heat Stock

This layer asks how to reduce exposure to already-accumulated heat.

It includes:

* ocean heat monitoring
* urban heat reduction
* surface cooling strategies
* water-cycle restoration
* thermal-risk mapping
* marine heatwave response
* reduction of peak heat stress

This is where the concept of **Direct Planetary Cooling** becomes relevant.

Direct Planetary Cooling should not be understood as replacing emissions reduction.
It is a proposed complementary layer for addressing accumulated heat and weakened cooling functions.

### Layer 3: Restore Circulation Functions

This includes:

* forest restoration
* soil regeneration
* microbial-cycle recovery
* wetland restoration
* water retention
* evapotranspiration recovery
* ocean circulation support
* plankton and marine ecosystem recovery
* carbon sink restoration

This layer aims to restore the Earth’s natural cooling, buffering, and carbon-fixation functions.

Direct Planetary Cooling is not simply an attempt to artificially cool the planet.

In this framework, it means:

The purpose is not to deny emissions reduction.

The purpose is to add a missing layer:

```text
emissions reduction

+ direct natural cooling support
```

## 12. What This Framework Changes

A conventional simplified view may say:

> Reduce CO2 emissions, and global warming will eventually stop.

The Planetary Heat and Circulation OS view says:

> Reducing CO2 emissions is necessary, but the Earth system also contains accumulated ocean heat, thermal inertia, weakened water cycles, degraded soils, stressed ecosystems, and weakened carbon sinks. Climate stabilization must address all of these together.

This is not a rejection of climate science.

It is an attempt to widen the frame from a single input variable to a whole-system stability model.

## 13. Public Claim Boundary

This framework should be described carefully.

Use:

> Planetary Heat and Circulation OS is a conceptual framework for understanding global warming as a combined problem of heat accumulation and weakened planetary circulation functions.

Use:

> It does not deny the role of greenhouse gases. It argues that emissions reduction must be combined with heat-stock awareness, carbon sink restoration, water-cycle recovery, ecosystem regeneration, and natural cooling support.

Avoid:

> CO2 does not matter.

Avoid:

> This framework proves that existing science is wrong.

Avoid:

> Direct Planetary Cooling replaces emissions reduction.

Prefer:

> Direct Planetary Cooling is a complementary proposal for addressing accumulated heat and weakened natural cooling functions while emissions reduction continues.
